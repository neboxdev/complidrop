# Federal obligations — Photographer / Videographer (event photo/video vendors)

Scope: Federal (United States) regulatory obligations for a business that provides
event photography and/or videography services. Categories: business/activity
licenses & permits, per-worker certifications, insurance mandated by law.

**Summary orientation (read first):** There is **no federal license, permit, or
occupational credential required to work as a photographer or videographer.**
Photography/videography is not a federally licensed occupation. The ONLY federal
obligations that attach are **conditional on a specific activity**: if the vendor
operates a **drone (small unmanned aircraft) commercially** to capture event
photos/video, FAA Part 107 applies (remote pilot certificate + aircraft
registration). Everything below is gated on that drone-operation trigger. If the
vendor does not fly drones, there are **zero** federal encodable obligations
(see the absence entry OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-000).

### OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-000: No federal license/credential to be a photographer or videographer (ABSENCE)

- **Applies to:** Every event photographer/videographer, unconditionally. This is
  the baseline absence finding: absent a triggering activity (drone flight — see
  001–003 below), a photo/video vendor has no federal license, permit, or
  occupational certification obligation.
- **Obligation:** None. Photography and videography are not federally regulated
  occupations. No federal agency issues an occupational license to "photographers"
  or "videographers," and no federal statute conditions the practice of
  photography/videography on holding a federal credential.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** N/A (no federal issuer exists). The absence is
  demonstrable from the structure of federal occupational regulation: federal
  occupational licensing is activity-specific (aviation → FAA, radio spectrum →
  FCC, firearms → ATF, etc.), and none of those regimes covers the act of taking
  photographs or recording video as such.
- **Cadence:** N/A.
- **Penalty for lapse:** N/A.
- **Category:** license
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** (Absence — no citable federal licensing statute exists for the
  occupation.) Corroborated by the SBA "Apply for licenses and permits" guidance,
  which frames federal licensing as required only for a short, enumerated list of
  federally regulated activities (agriculture, alcohol, aviation, firearms/
  ammunition/explosives, fish & wildlife, commercial fisheries, maritime
  transportation, mining/drilling, nuclear energy, radio/TV broadcasting,
  transportation & logistics) — photography/videography is not among them. —
  https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/apply-licenses-permits
- **Operative text:** From SBA (fetched verbatim): "Most small businesses need a
  combination of licenses and permits from both federal and state agencies.
  Business activities regulated by a federal agency will need a federal license or
  permit." The page then enumerates the federally-regulated activities:
  agriculture; alcoholic beverages; aviation; firearms, ammunition, and
  explosives; fish and wildlife; commercial fisheries; maritime transportation;
  mining and drilling; nuclear energy; radio and television broadcasting;
  transportation and logistics. Photography/videography is absent from the list.
  *(SBA is a federal .gov agency; it is used here as an official structural source
  for the absence, not as the licensing authority.)*
- **Effective date of cited text:** current as displayed (fetched 2026-07-07).
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** Absence is bounded to the OCCUPATION. Activity-conditional federal
  obligations still apply if the vendor performs a regulated activity — the only
  one realistically in scope for event photo/video is commercial drone operation
  (001–003). Music licensing / sync rights for wedding video (ASCAP/BMI, 17 U.S.C.
  copyright) is a private-royalty / contractual matter, not a government-issued
  license or permit, and is out of encoding scope. No per-worker federal
  certification exists for camera operators.

### OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-001: FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate (commercial drone operation)

- **Applies to:** A photo/video vendor **only if** it operates a small unmanned
  aircraft system (drone, < 55 lb) to capture images/video for its business
  (a commercial/non-recreational purpose). The person manipulating the flight
  controls must either hold the certificate or fly under the direct supervision of
  a certificate holder. Trigger = commercial drone flight. If the vendor never
  flies a drone, this obligation does not attach.
- **Obligation:** A **Remote Pilot Certificate with a small UAS rating** issued
  under 14 CFR part 107, subpart C. Obtained by passing the initial aeronautical
  knowledge test ("Unmanned Aircraft General – Small," UAG) at an FAA-approved
  testing center and applying via IACRA; applicant must be at least 16 and vetted
  by TSA. (The physical/plastic certificate is issued after TSA security
  vetting.)
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** Federal Aviation Administration (FAA).
- **Cadence:** The certificate itself does **not** expire. However, to exercise
  its privileges the holder must satisfy an ongoing **aeronautical knowledge
  recency** requirement every **24 calendar months** — see
  OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-002. (This is the renewal-like recurring obligation the
  compliance engine should track, NOT a certificate expiry date.)
- **Penalty for lapse:** Operating without a required remote pilot certificate is
  a violation of the FAA regulations enforceable under 49 U.S.C. and 14 CFR;
  FAA may pursue civil penalties and certificate action. (Penalty amounts not
  quoted from a fetched primary source here — see Open questions.)
- **Category:** worker-certification
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** 14 CFR 107.12 (certificate requirement); 14 CFR 107.61
  (eligibility); part 107 subpart C (issuance). URLs fetched:
  https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CFR-2024-title14-vol2/xml/CFR-2024-title14-vol2-sec107-12.xml
  (GPO official CFR text) and https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/107.12 ,
  https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/107.61 . Canonical eCFR location:
  https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107/subpart-B/section-107.12
  (eCFR blocked WebFetch with a 302 to unblock.federalregister.gov — text verified
  via GPO govinfo + Cornell instead).
- **Operative text:** 14 CFR 107.12(a): "Except as provided in paragraph (c) of
  this section, no person may manipulate the flight controls of a small unmanned
  aircraft system unless: (1) That person has a remote pilot certificate with a
  small UAS rating issued pursuant to subpart C of this part and satisfies the
  requirements of § 107.65; or (2) That person is under the direct supervision of
  a remote pilot in command and the remote pilot in command has the ability to
  immediately take direct control of the flight of the small unmanned aircraft."
  107.12(b): "Except as provided in paragraph (c) of this section, no person may
  act as a remote pilot in command unless that person has a remote pilot
  certificate with a small UAS rating issued pursuant to Subpart C of this part
  and satisfies the requirements of § 107.65." 107.61(a): a person must "Be at
  least 16 years of age."
- **Effective date of cited text:** current as displayed — 2024 CFR annual edition
  (GPO govinfo, title 14 vol. 2). The "Except as provided in paragraph (c)" clause
  and paragraph (c) (foreign-registered aircraft) are present in the current text.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** Do NOT confuse with **TRUST** (The Recreational UAS Safety Test),
  which is for RECREATIONAL flyers only (49 U.S.C. 44809 exception) and does NOT
  authorize commercial/for-hire drone work — a paid event photo/video shoot is
  commercial and requires Part 107, never TRUST. See OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-002 for
  the recency cadence. Direct-supervision path (a)(2) lets an uncertificated
  camera operator fly if a Part 107 certificate holder is the remote PIC and can
  immediately take control — relevant for multi-operator shoots.

### OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-002: FAA aeronautical knowledge recency — every 24 calendar months (drone)

- **Applies to:** Any Part 107 remote pilot in command who continues to fly a
  drone commercially for the vendor (i.e., the same drone-operation trigger as
  001). This is the recurring obligation attached to the certificate.
- **Obligation:** Within the previous **24 calendar months**, the remote pilot
  must have completed (a) the initial aeronautical knowledge test, or (b) the
  **free online recurrent training** ("Part 107 Small UAS Recurrent" on FAA
  Training / faasafety.gov), or (c) for part 61 certificate holders, the § 107.74
  training. Failing to keep recency current means the pilot **may not exercise
  remote-PIC privileges** until recency is restored (the certificate is not
  revoked; the privilege is suspended). Since April 6, 2021 this is a free online
  course, not a proctored retest.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** Federal Aviation Administration (FAA).
- **Cadence:** Rolling **24 calendar months** from the most recent qualifying
  test/training completion. This IS the date the compliance engine should track
  for a drone-flying photo/video vendor.
- **Penalty for lapse:** Not a fine per se; the pilot loses authority to act as
  remote PIC until recency is re-established. Flying without current recency =
  operating without satisfying § 107.12/§ 107.65 (enforceable as an FAA
  violation).
- **Category:** worker-certification
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** 14 CFR 107.65 (Aeronautical knowledge recency). URL fetched:
  https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/107.65 . Canonical eCFR:
  https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107/subpart-C/section-107.65
  (eCFR blocked WebFetch; text verified via Cornell LII, which shows the amendment
  citation).
- **Operative text:** 14 CFR 107.65: "A person may not exercise the privileges of
  a remote pilot in command with small UAS rating unless that person has
  accomplished one of the following in a manner acceptable to the Administrator
  within the previous 24 calendar months: (a) Passed an initial aeronautical
  knowledge test covering the areas of knowledge specified in § 107.73; (b)
  Completed recurrent training covering the areas of knowledge specified in
  § 107.73; or (c) If a person holds a pilot certificate (other than a student
  pilot certificate) issued under part 61 of this chapter and meets the flight
  review requirements specified in § 61.56, completed training covering the areas
  of knowledge specified in § 107.74."
- **Effective date of cited text:** Amdt. 107-8, 86 FR 4382, Jan. 15, 2021; 86 FR
  13631, Mar. 10, 2021 (the amendment that replaced the biennial recurrent TEST
  with the current recurrent TRAINING recency model, effective April 6, 2021).
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** This is the single recurring date that matters for a drone-operating
  photo/video vendor. Certificate = permanent; recency = every 24 calendar months.
  Because the source anchors recency to "the previous 24 calendar months" of a
  test/training completion, the renewal anchor is the completion date, and it runs
  to the end of the 24th calendar month.

### OBL-FED-PHOTOGRAPHER-003: FAA small-UAS (drone) registration — Part 48, 3-year term (commercial)

- **Applies to:** A photo/video vendor that operates a drone commercially — **each**
  small unmanned aircraft (drone) it flies must be registered, regardless of
  weight. (The sub-0.55 lb / 250 g registration exemption applies ONLY to drones
  flown *exclusively* recreationally under 49 U.S.C. 44809; a drone flown for a
  paid shoot under Part 107 must be registered even if it weighs under 250 g.)
  Trigger = commercial drone flight, same as 001–002.
- **Obligation:** Register each aircraft with the FAA (obtain a Certificate of
  Aircraft Registration via the FAA DroneZone / registration system), pay the fee,
  and mark the aircraft with its registration number. Registration is per aircraft
  for non-recreational operations (a single certificate does NOT cover a whole
  fleet the way the recreational registration does).
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** Federal Aviation Administration (FAA).
- **Cadence:** Registration **expires 3 years after the date of issue unless
  renewed.** Renewal: the holder verifies information is current within the six
  months before expiration; a renewed certificate expires three years from the
  prior expiration date. Fee **$5.00 per aircraft** (issuance or renewal).
- **Penalty for lapse:** Operating an unregistered aircraft that requires
  registration violates 14 CFR 48.15 / 49 U.S.C. 44101–44103; civil and (for
  knowing violations) criminal penalties are available under 49 U.S.C. (amounts
  not quoted from a fetched primary source here — see Open questions).
- **Category:** permit
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** 14 CFR 48.15 (requirement to register); 14 CFR 48.100
  (registration for aircraft operated for any purpose other than exclusively
  limited recreational operations — i.e., commercial/Part 107); 14 CFR 48.30
  (fee). URLs fetched: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/48.15 ,
  https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/48.100 , https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/48.30 .
- **Operative text:** 14 CFR 48.15: "No person may operate a small unmanned
  aircraft that is eligible for registration under 49 U.S.C. 44101–44103 unless
  one of the following criteria has been satisfied: (a) The owner has registered
  and marked the aircraft in accordance with this part; (b) The aircraft is
  operated exclusively in compliance with 49 U.S.C. 44809 and weighs 0.55 pounds
  or less on takeoff, including everything that is on board or otherwise attached
  to the aircraft; or (c) The aircraft is an aircraft of the Armed Forces of the
  United States." 14 CFR 48.100 (heading): "Registration: Small unmanned aircraft
  operated for any purpose other than exclusively limited recreational
  operations," providing "A Certificate of Aircraft registration issued under this
  part expires 3 years after the date of issue unless it is renewed." 14 CFR
  48.30: "The fee for issuing or renewing a Certificate of Aircraft Registration
  as described in § 48.100 is $5.00 per aircraft."
- **Effective date of cited text:** current as displayed (Cornell LII reproduction
  of the current CFR; fetched 2026-07-07).
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** This is a per-aircraft registration, so a vendor with multiple drones
  registers (and renews) each one on its own 3-year clock. Distinct from the
  remote-pilot recency clock in 002. The FAA operational marking rule (14 CFR
  48.205 — display of the registration number on the exterior) is a related
  requirement, not separately encoded. FAA plain-language page
  (faa.gov/uas/getting_started/register_drone) 403'd on WebFetch; the term/fee
  were verified directly from the CFR text instead.

## Local-level obligations (noted, not encoded)

(Federal file — this section is for completeness; local obligations are captured
in the Texas file and are inherently sub-federal.)

- **Municipal / county film & photography permits.** Many U.S. cities and
  counties (and the National Park Service / federal land units, and some venues)
  require a **film/photo permit** to shoot commercially on public property, in
  parks, or in special locations. This is a location-and-property matter set by
  the land manager, not a federal occupational license, and it varies by
  jurisdiction — check the specific city/county/park where the event occurs.
- **Federal land photography permits (NPS/USFS/BLM).** Commercial
  filming/photography on federal lands can require a permit and fees under agency
  rules (e.g., National Park Service commercial filming permits). Triggered only
  if an event shoot occurs on federal land; not a general occupational obligation.
  Noted, not encoded.
