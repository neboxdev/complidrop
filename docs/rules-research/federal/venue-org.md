# Federal obligations — VENUE-ORG (Texas event venue business itself)

Entity: the event-venue business itself (CompliDrop's customer). A Texas event
venue that may serve/sell alcohol and food. Federal jurisdiction only in this
file; Texas obligations live in `../texas/venue-org.md`.

Headline: **an ordinary event venue has almost no federal document obligations.**
The one-time EIN and (if it serves alcohol) a one-time TTB dealer registration
are the only affirmative federal filings; OSHA injury-log recordkeeping is
document-shaped only above an employee-count threshold. There is **no federal
business license** for an event venue.

---

### OBL-FED-VENUE-001: Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN) — one-time, no renewal

- **Applies to:** Any venue that has employees, or is organized as a
  partnership, LLC, or corporation, or must pay employment/excise/alcohol
  taxes. (A single-owner venue with no employees taxed as a sole proprietor is
  not strictly required to have one, but effectively always obtains one to bank
  and to hold a TABC permit.)
- **Obligation:** Obtain an EIN from the IRS (Form SS-4 / online). This is a
  registration/identifier, **not a license or a recurring filing**.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** Internal Revenue Service (IRS).
- **Cadence:** **One-time.** An EIN, once assigned, is the entity's permanent
  federal taxpayer ID; it does not expire and is not renewed. (A new EIN is
  needed only on a change of entity structure/ownership — see IRS "When to get a
  new EIN.") Record as a standing identifier, NOT a renewal rule.
- **Penalty for lapse:** N/A (no renewal). Operating without one when required
  blocks payroll/tax filing rather than triggering a discrete permit penalty.
- **Category:** filing (one-time registration)
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** IRC §6109; IRS EIN page
  https://www.irs.gov/businesses/employer-identification-number ; IRS Pub. 1635
  https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p1635.pdf
- **Operative text:** IRS — "An EIN is a federal tax ID number for businesses,
  tax-exempt organizations and other entities." / "You need an EIN if you: Have
  employees; Will need to pay employment, excise or alcohol, tobacco, and
  firearms taxes; Withhold taxes on income, other than wages, paid to a
  non-resident alien." / needed to operate a "Partnership, Limited liability
  company (LLC), Corporation, Tax-exempt organization, Estate, Trust, Retirement
  plan…".
- **Effective date of cited text:** Current as displayed 2026-07-07.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** The "permanent / never expires / never reused" fact is stated in
  IRS Pub. 1635 ("EINs are not reused or reassigned … the permanent Federal
  taxpayer identification number for that entity") — the live EIN webpage I
  fetched did not restate it verbatim (it only says the IRS can deactivate but
  not cancel an EIN), so the permanence quote is cited to Pub. 1635 rather than
  quoted here. Included because the lead asked to record EIN as a note/absence,
  not a cadence rule — this entry is deliberately NOT a reminder-generating
  obligation.

### OBL-FED-VENUE-002: No federal business license for an ordinary event venue (ABSENCE)

- **Applies to:** The event-venue business as such (renting space, hosting
  events, in-house food/drink service). This entry records the *absence* of any
  federal licensing requirement so the engine can distinguish "no obligation"
  from "not researched."
- **Obligation:** **None at the federal level** for the core venue business.
  Federal licenses attach only to specific *federally regulated activities*,
  none of which an ordinary venue performs. (If the venue sells alcohol, the
  only federal touchpoint is the TTB dealer *registration* in OBL-FED-VENUE-003
  — a registration, not a license, and TTB issues no federal permit to a
  *retail* on-premise seller.)
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** N/A (various federal agencies for the listed
  regulated activities; none applicable here).
- **Cadence:** N/A.
- **Penalty for lapse:** N/A.
- **Category:** license (absence finding)
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** U.S. SBA, "Apply for licenses and permits"
  https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/apply-licenses-permits
- **Operative text:** SBA — "Business activities regulated by a federal agency
  will need a federal license or permit." The federally regulated activities the
  SBA enumerates are: **Agriculture; Alcoholic beverages (Manufacture,
  wholesale, import, or retail sale); Aviation; Firearms, ammunition, and
  explosives; Fish and wildlife; Commercial fisheries; Maritime transportation;
  Mining and drilling; Nuclear energy; Radio and television broadcasting;
  Transportation and logistics** (oversize/overweight vehicles). An event venue
  performs none of the manufacture/wholesale/import/aviation/firearms/etc.
  activities.
- **Effective date of cited text:** Current as displayed 2026-07-07.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** The SBA list does say "Alcoholic beverages … retail sale," but that
  general phrasing collapses to the TTB *registration* (not a permit) covered in
  003 — TTB requires a federal *basic permit* only of producers, importers, and
  wholesalers under the Federal Alcohol Administration Act, not of on-premise
  retailers. So the practical federal-license count for a venue that serves
  drinks is still zero.

### OBL-FED-VENUE-003: TTB alcohol dealer registration (Form 5630.5d) — if the venue sells alcohol

- **Applies to:** Any venue that sells or offers to sell distilled spirits,
  wine, or beer at retail for on-premise consumption (i.e. any venue holding a
  TABC MB or BG permit and pouring drinks). One registration per business
  location.
- **Obligation:** Register with TTB as an alcohol dealer by filing **TTB Form
  5630.5d (Alcohol Dealer Registration)** before commencing business. **No
  federal permit is required** for a retail dealer, and **no special
  (occupational) tax is due** (that tax was repealed effective July 1, 2008).
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** U.S. Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade
  Bureau (TTB), Treasury.
- **Cadence:** Effectively **one-time**: file before engaging in business "and
  on or before July 1 of each year thereafter" — BUT re-filing is required only
  if the registered information changes; if nothing has changed, no further
  registration is required. So in practice a venue files once and re-files only
  on a change (location, ownership, etc.).
- **Penalty for lapse:** Failure to register/keep required records is a
  violation of 26 U.S.C. ch. 51 / 27 CFR pt. 31 (penalties for dealing without
  registration; recordkeeping violations).
- **Category:** filing (one-time registration)
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** 27 CFR pt. 31 (Alcohol Beverage Dealers), subpart G (Form
  5630.5d); requirement at 27 CFR §31.111. Verbatim text obtained from the
  Cornell LII CFR mirror https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/27/31.111 ;
  repeal/registration background: Federal Register, "Liquor Dealer Recordkeeping
  and Registration, and Repeal of Certain Special (Occupational) Taxes" (74 FR
  37402, 2009-07-28)
  https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2009/07/28/E9-17177/ ; TTB guidance
  https://www.ttb.gov/nrc/retail-beverage-alcohol-dealers
- **Operative text:** 27 CFR §31.111(a) — "Dealers must register by filing the
  registration form, TTB Form 5630.5d, before engaging in business and on or
  before July 1 of each year thereafter. However, as long as none of the
  information specified on the form has changed since the previous registration
  form was filed, no additional registration is required."
- **Effective date of cited text:** Current CFR (part 31 as revised after the
  2008 special-tax repeal).
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** probable
- **Notes:** Downgraded to `probable` ONLY because the primary federal host
  could not be fetched live on 2026-07-07: **eCFR (ecfr.gov) 302-redirected to
  an anti-bot "unblock" gateway**, **govinfo.gov (GPO) 302-redirected via curl**,
  and **ttb.gov timed out on three attempts**. The operative sentence is quoted
  verbatim from the Cornell LII CFR reproduction, which is a faithful copy of the
  official text, and is triple-corroborated by TTB guidance and the Federal
  Register repeal notice. A future run that reaches eCFR/ttb.gov should upgrade
  this to `verified` unchanged. Practically this is a trivial one-time paper step
  a venue does at opening, not a recurring compliance-reminder item.

### OBL-FED-VENUE-004: OSHA injury & illness recordkeeping (OSHA 300/300A/301) — at 11+ employees

- **Applies to:** A venue that had **11 or more employees at any time during the
  last calendar year** AND is **not** in a partially exempt industry. The common
  event-venue NAICS codes are **NOT** on OSHA's partial-exemption list, so an
  11+-employee venue is generally covered (see Notes on classification). A venue
  with **10 or fewer employees** all year is exempt from the routine logs.
- **Obligation:** Maintain the **OSHA Form 300** (Log of Work-Related Injuries
  and Illnesses), **Form 301** (Incident Report), and post the **Form 300A**
  annual summary. Document-shaped: the 300 log, 301 reports, 300A summary.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** U.S. Occupational Safety and Health
  Administration (OSHA), DOL. (Texas has no state OSHA plan for private
  employers — federal OSHA applies directly.)
- **Cadence:** Continuous logging; retain records **5 years**. **Post the 300A
  summary February 1 through April 30 each year** (29 CFR 1904.32). Certain
  larger establishments must also electronically submit the 300A (and 300/301)
  annually by March 2 (29 CFR 1904.41) — unlikely for a small venue; see Notes.
- **Penalty for lapse:** OSHA recordkeeping citations (per-violation penalties
  under the OSH Act, adjusted annually for inflation).
- **Category:** filing (recordkeeping + annual posting)
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** 29 CFR 1904.1 (size partial exemption), 1904.2 (industry partial
  exemption) + Non-Mandatory Appendix A to Subpart B. Verbatim from OSHA agency
  pages: https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1904/1904.1 ,
  https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1904/1904.2 ,
  exempt-industry table
  https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/presentations/exempttable
- **Operative text:** 1904.1(a)(1) — "If your company had 10 or fewer employees
  at all times during the last calendar year, you do not need to keep OSHA injury
  and illness records unless OSHA or the Bureau of Labor Statistics informs you in
  writing that you must keep records." 1904.2(a)(1) — "If your business
  establishment is classified in a specific industry group listed in appendix A
  to this subpart, you do not need to keep OSHA injury and illness records unless
  the government asks you to keep the records under §1904.41 or §1904.42."
- **Effective date of cited text:** Current CFR as displayed on osha.gov
  2026-07-07.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes on NAICS classification (the applicability hinge):** OSHA's partial-
  exemption Appendix A / table was checked on osha.gov for the codes an event
  venue plausibly carries. **NOT exempt (⇒ must keep the log at 11+):** 531120
  Lessors of Nonresidential Buildings (space-rental venues), 7139/713990 Other
  Amusement & Recreation, 722320 Caterers / 7223 Special Food Services (in-house
  catering venues). **Exempt (⇒ no log even at 11+):** 7221 Full-Service
  Restaurants and 7222 Limited-Service Eating Places DO appear on the list, so a
  venue whose primary classification is a restaurant would be exempt. Because the
  typical banquet/event-venue codes are NOT exempt, the default rule for the
  engine is: **event venue + 11 or more employees ⇒ OSHA 300 log required.** If a
  particular venue is BLS/NAICS-classified as a restaurant (7221/7222) it is
  exempt — a per-venue detail worth surfacing rather than hard-coding. Regardless
  of size or industry, **all** employers must still *report* to OSHA any
  work-related fatality (within 8 hours) or in-patient hospitalization/amputation/
  loss of an eye (within 24 hours) — that is an event-driven report, not a
  standing document, so it is noted here but not encoded as a document obligation.
  Electronic submission (1904.41) generally starts at 250 employees, or 20–249 in
  designated high-hazard industries (Appendix A to Subpart E — a different list);
  a small SMB venue is below these thresholds, so e-submission is flagged for
  review rather than encoded.

## Local-level obligations (noted, not encoded)

None at the federal level (this file is federal-only). All venue *place-of-
business* permits — certificate of occupancy, fire marshal/assembly, local
health, noise/sign — are municipal/county and are catalogued in the Texas file's
"Local-level obligations" section. No federal analog exists for those.
