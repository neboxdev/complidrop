# Federal regulatory obligations — Event Rental Company

Entity: **event-rental** (tables, tents, chairs, staging, equipment, and
inflatables / bounce houses rented to event venues). Jurisdiction level:
**federal**. Market: United States only.

> **Summary:** There is **no federal license to run an event-rental / inflatable
> business** and **no federal document obligation on a rental operator from the
> CPSC** (product-safety certification falls on manufacturers/importers, not the
> renter). The one real, conditional federal obligation is **OSHA powered-
> industrial-truck (forklift) operator certification**, which applies only if
> the company operates forklifts / telehandlers / rider pallet jacks in its
> warehouse or yard. (Texas private employers are under **federal OSHA** — Texas
> has no OSHA-approved State Plan for the private sector — so 1910.178 applies
> directly.)

---

### OBL-FED-EVENT-001: OSHA powered-industrial-truck (forklift) operator training, evaluation & certification

- **Applies to:** Any event-rental employer whose workers operate a **powered
  industrial truck** — forklifts, telehandlers/reach lifts, order pickers, and
  motorized/rider **pallet jacks** — in the warehouse or loading yard.
  **Conditional:** if the company handles inventory purely by hand, hand trucks,
  or non-powered pallet jacks, this obligation is **not triggered**. Applies per
  operator, regardless of how infrequently they drive.
- **Obligation:** Before an employee operates a PIT, the **employer must train
  and evaluate** the operator and **certify** the training/evaluation in a
  written record. Refresher training is required on defined triggers (accident,
  near-miss, observed unsafe operation, reassignment to a different truck type,
  or a workplace change). The certification record (name, training date,
  evaluation date, evaluator identity) must be kept.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** U.S. Department of Labor — Occupational
  Safety and Health Administration (OSHA). In Texas, **federal** OSHA (no state
  plan).
- **Cadence:** **Initial** — before first operation. **Performance evaluation —
  at least once every three years** per operator. **Refresher training** —
  event-triggered (no fixed interval). Certification is a retained record, not a
  periodically renewed license.
- **Penalty for lapse:** OSHA citation. Civil penalties under the OSH Act
  (29 U.S.C. §666) for other-than-serious, serious, repeat, and willful
  violations; statutory maximums are adjusted annually for inflation (exact
  current dollar figures **not fetched this session** — do not quote a number
  without verifying 29 CFR §1903.15 as adjusted).
- **Category:** worker-certification
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** 29 CFR §1910.178(l) (Operator training) — subparagraphs
  (l)(1)(ii), (l)(4)(iii), (l)(6).
  - https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1910/1910.178
- **Operative text:**
  - §1910.178(l)(1)(ii): "**Prior to permitting an employee to operate a powered
    industrial truck (except for training purposes), the employer shall ensure
    that each operator has successfully completed the training required by this
    paragraph (l)** …"
  - §1910.178(l)(4)(iii): "**An evaluation of each powered industrial truck
    operator's performance shall be conducted at least once every three
    years.**"
  - §1910.178(l)(6) (Certification): "**The employer shall certify that each
    operator has been trained and evaluated as required by this paragraph (l).
    The certification shall include the name of the operator, the date of the
    training, the date of the evaluation, and the identity of the person(s)
    performing the training or evaluation.**"
- **Effective date of cited text:** Standard in force since the 1998 final rule;
  current as displayed on osha.gov.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** This is an **employer**-held certification (a training record), not
  a government-issued operator license — there is no OSHA "forklift card"
  registry; third-party "certification" courses only supply part of the training
  the employer must still finalize with a workplace-specific evaluation. Texas
  has **no** OSHA State Plan covering private employers, so federal 1910.178
  governs Texas event-rental warehouses directly (confirm state-plan status if a
  reviewer wants a fetched cite; well-established). Highly relevant to CompliDrop
  as a per-worker credential the vendor can be asked to evidence.

### OBL-FED-EVENT-002: (Absence) No federal license to operate an event-rental / inflatable-rental business

- **Applies to:** All event-rental companies (tables, tents, chairs, staging,
  equipment, inflatables).
- **Obligation:** **None.** There is no general federal business license, and
  event-equipment / tent / inflatable rental is **not** among the federally
  regulated activities that require an agency license. Federal licensing is
  activity-specific (alcohol, aviation, firearms/explosives, wildlife,
  agriculture, maritime, mining/drilling, nuclear, radio/TV broadcasting,
  oversize-vehicle transport) — none of which a plain event-rental operation
  performs by renting party goods.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** N/A (absence). SBA catalogs the federally
  regulated activities.
- **Cadence:** N/A.
- **Penalty for lapse:** N/A.
- **Category:** license (absence finding)
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** U.S. Small Business Administration — "Apply for licenses and
  permits."
  - https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/apply-licenses-permits
- **Operative text:** SBA: "**Most small businesses need a combination of
  licenses and permits from both federal and state agencies. Business activities
  regulated by a federal agency will need a federal license or permit.**" The
  enumerated federal activities (agriculture, alcoholic beverages, aviation,
  firearms/ammunition/explosives, fish & wildlife, commercial fisheries,
  maritime transportation, mining & drilling, nuclear energy, radio & TV
  broadcasting, transportation/logistics of oversize/overweight vehicles) **do
  not include event-equipment, tent, or inflatable rental.**
- **Effective date of cited text:** Current as displayed on sba.gov.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified
- **Notes:** Absence-is-a-finding entry. A rental company that *adds* a
  federally regulated activity (e.g., selling alcohol at events, operating a
  large commercial fleet interstate) picks up the corresponding federal
  obligation — see the DOT/FMCSA cross-reference below.

## Cross-references & federal notes (not encoded as operator obligations)

- **CPSC (Consumer Product Safety Commission) — no operator document.**
  Inflatables and party equipment are "consumer products" within CPSC's reach,
  and CPSC can set safety standards / order recalls. But the **certification
  duty runs to manufacturers and importers, not to a business that rents
  already-manufactured units.** Per **15 U.S.C. §2063(a)(1)**: "**Every
  manufacturer of a product which is subject to a consumer product safety rule
  under this chapter … and which is imported for consumption or warehoused or
  distributed in commerce (and the private labeler of such product if such
  product bears a private label) shall issue a certificate** …" A downstream
  renter is **not** a manufacturer/importer/private labeler and issues **no**
  General Certificate of Conformity. ⇒ **Note, not an entry.** (Read at
  https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/2063 ; CPSC.gov pages returned 403
  to automated fetch — statute used as the primary anchor.) A rental operator
  should still honor any CPSC **recall** on units it owns, but that is a product
  duty, not a license/credential CompliDrop would track.
- **DOT / FMCSA — conditional on operating commercial motor vehicles.** If the
  company runs delivery trucks meeting CMV thresholds (e.g., GVWR/GVW ≥ 10,001
  lb in interstate commerce, or Texas intrastate equivalents), USDOT-number,
  driver-qualification, and related obligations attach. **Do not duplicate
  here** — see the **transportation** entity research. Treat as conditional for
  event-rental companies with their own fleet.
- **Federal EIN / employment tax forms.** Out of research scope (W-9 / tax forms
  deferred per METHODOLOGY). Noted only so the engine doesn't treat the gap as
  "unresearched."
- **OSHA general-industry standards beyond forklifts** (e.g., PPE, hazard
  communication, walking-working surfaces) apply to the warehouse as a workplace
  but are **program/condition** duties, not per-worker credentials or licenses,
  so they are not encoded as document obligations here.

## Local-level obligations (noted, not encoded)

- Not applicable at the **federal** level — the federal government issues no
  municipal-style local permits. Local (city/county) obligations for an
  event-rental company (tent permits, warehouse business license, certificate of
  occupancy, local fire inspection of inflatables) are catalogued in the
  **Texas** dossier (`texas/event-rental.md`) under its Local-level section.
