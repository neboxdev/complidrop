# Texas obligations — Photographer / Videographer (event photo/video vendors)

Scope: State of Texas regulatory obligations for a business that provides event
photography and/or videography services. Categories: business/activity licenses &
permits, per-worker certifications, insurance mandated by law. Federal obligations
(FAA Part 107 for commercial drone work) live in the federal file.

**Summary orientation (read first):** Texas has **no occupational license,
certification, or permit to work as a photographer or videographer.** Texas does
not even have a *general* business license. The only Texas "permits" the official
state guide associates with the word "photography" are **environmental permits
tied to chemical PHOTO-FINISHING / darkroom operations** (film development), which
a modern **digital** event photo/video vendor does not perform — so for a typical
event photographer/videographer the Texas encodable-obligation set is **empty**.
Sales-tax registration and (elective) workers' comp are cross-referenced below but
are owned by the `venue-org` dossier, not encoded here. The Texas drone statute
(Gov't Code ch. 423) is a criminal *conduct* law, not a licensing scheme.

### OBL-TX-PHOTOGRAPHER-000: No Texas occupational license/permit to be a photographer or videographer (ABSENCE)

- **Applies to:** Every event photographer/videographer operating in Texas,
  unconditionally. Baseline absence finding for the occupation.
- **Obligation:** None. Texas does not license or register "photographers" or
  "videographers" as an occupation, and Texas imposes no *general* business
  license. No Texas agency issues an occupational credential to photograph or
  record video for hire.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** N/A — no Texas issuer exists. Demonstrated by
  three official Texas sources: (1) TDLR, the state's umbrella occupational-
  licensing agency, does not list photography/videography among its regulated
  programs; (2) the Office of the Governor's Business Permit Office guide contains
  no photographer/videographer occupational license (its "Photography / Photo
  Finishing" heading lists only environmental permits — see OBL-TX-PHOTOGRAPHER-001);
  (3) the Governor's "Start a Business" page states Texas has no general business
  license.
- **Cadence:** N/A.
- **Penalty for lapse:** N/A.
- **Category:** license
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:**
  - TDLR "Programs Licensed and Regulated by TDLR" —
    https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/licenses.htm (fetched 2026-07-07).
  - Office of the Governor, "Start a Business in Texas" —
    https://gov.texas.gov/business/page/start-a-business (fetched 2026-07-07).
  - Office of the Governor, Business Permit Office, *2026-2027 Texas Business
    Licenses & Permits Guide* (updated December 2025) —
    https://gov.texas.gov/uploads/files/business/Texas_Licenses_Permits_Guide.pdf
    (downloaded and text-extracted 2026-07-07; the "PHOTOGRAPHY / PHOTO FINISHING"
    entry is on p. 105).
- **Operative text:**
  - gov.texas.gov (Start a Business): "A general business license is not required
    in Texas." and "However, it is important to determine necessary licenses,
    permits, certifications, registrations or authorizations for a specific
    business activity, at the federal, state and local level."
  - TDLR list header: "Programs Licensed and Regulated by TDLR" — the enumerated

  "UPDATED DECEMBER 2025." Fetched/extracted 2026-07-07.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** verified

  employment obligations exist but are venue-org-owned cross-references (below).

  event videographer is NOT a "private investigator" and does not fall under the
  DPS Private Security regime (Occupations Code ch. 1702) merely by recording
  events — that chapter targets investigative/guard services, not event
  videography.

### OBL-TX-PHOTOGRAPHER-001: TCEQ environmental permits — ONLY for chemical photo-finishing (conditional; near-nil for digital event vendors)

- **Applies to:** A photo business **only if** it operates a chemical
  **photo-finishing / darkroom** process (traditional film development) that emits
  air contaminants or generates industrial/hazardous wastewater. **This does NOT
  apply to digital event photography/videography** — a vendor that shoots and
  delivers digital images/video and does no on-site chemical film processing has
  **no** obligation here. Included to disambiguate: the only Texas "permits" the
  official guide files under "photography" are these environmental ones, and they
  are triggered by the *photo-finishing* activity, not by photography itself.
- **Obligation:** Depending on the process, one or more Texas Commission on
  Environmental Quality (TCEQ) authorizations: an **Air Permit by Rule**, plus
  compliance with **Industrial and Hazardous Waste**, **Industrial Wastewater and
  Stormwater**, and **Pollution Prevention Planning** regulations.
- **Issuing/enforcing authority:** Texas Commission on Environmental Quality
  (TCEQ).
- **Cadence:** Not established from a fetched primary TCEQ rule in this session
  (varies by authorization type). Do not assume a cadence.
- **Penalty for lapse:** TCEQ environmental enforcement (not quoted from a fetched
  primary source here).
- **Category:** permit
- **Basis:** regulatory
- **Citation:** *2026-2027 Texas Business Licenses & Permits Guide* (Office of the
  Governor, Business Permit Office), "PHOTOGRAPHY / PHOTO FINISHING," p. 105 —
  https://gov.texas.gov/uploads/files/business/Texas_Licenses_Permits_Guide.pdf
  (downloaded/extracted 2026-07-07). The underlying TCEQ rule chapters were NOT
  fetched/quoted this session.
- **Operative text:** From the guide's "PHOTOGRAPHY / PHOTO FINISHING" entry
  (verbatim listing): "Licenses and Permits: Air Permits by Rule - Texas Commission
  on Environmental Quality … Regulations: Industrial and Hazardous Waste
  Regulations - Texas Commission on Environmental Quality … Industrial Wastewater
  and Stormwater Regulations - Texas Commission on Environmental Quality …
  Pollution Prevention Planning - Texas Commission on Environment Quality …."
  (No occupational license appears under this heading.) *NOTE: this is the state
  guide's listing, not the controlling TCEQ regulatory sentence — the operative
  TCEQ rule text was not fetched, so confidence is held at `probable`.*
- **Effective date of cited text:** guide edition 2026-2027, updated December 2025.
- **Verified:** 2026-07-07 | **Confidence:** probable
- **Notes:** Realistically inapplicable to CompliDrop's target (digital event
  photo/video). Recommend the engine treat this as informational/gated behind an
  explicit "do you develop film chemically on-site?" trigger, never surfaced by
  default for an event photographer/videographer — surfacing a TCEQ air permit to
  a wedding photographer would be a false positive. Kept `probable` because the

## Local-level obligations (noted, not encoded)

- **Municipal/county film & photography permits.** Texas cities and counties may
  require a **film/photo shoot permit** (and sometimes insurance) to shoot
  commercially on public property, in city parks, or on closed streets — e.g.,
  city film offices in Austin, Dallas, Houston, San Antonio. This is set by the
  local land manager, varies by jurisdiction, and is out of v1 encoding scope.
  Check the specific city/county where the event occurs.
- **State-park photography permits.** Texas Parks & Wildlife requires a permit/fee
  for **commercial** photography/filming in state parks. Triggered only if an
  event shoot occurs in a Texas state park; noted, not encoded.
- **General local business registration.** Texas has no general state business
  license, but a vendor still registers its entity/assumed name with the Texas
  Secretary of State or county clerk (see cross-references). Local jurisdictions
  may impose their own registrations — "check your city/county."

## Cross-references (owned by other dossiers — NOT encoded here)

- **Texas Sales & Use Tax Permit (venue-org owns the canonical entry).**
  Photographers/videographers selling photographs, prints, digital images, or
  event photography services in Texas generally must hold a **Texas Sales and Use
  Tax Permit** and collect sales tax. Per the Texas Comptroller: "Sales of
  photographs are taxable," including images "sold as prints, on a DVD, CD,
  digitally, electronically or by any other medium," and a photographer billing
  sitting fees "must collect sales tax on the entire amount charged." Authority:
  Texas Comptroller, Publication 94-176 "Photographers and Texas Sales Tax"
  (comptroller.texas.gov; located via the Comptroller site 2026-07-07 — the
  94-176.php deep link 404'd, content confirmed via the Comptroller's own search
  result summary). Cross-referenced for completeness; the sales-tax-permit
  obligation is encoded once, under `venue-org`.
- **Workers' compensation is ELECTIVE in Texas (venue-org owns it).** Texas is the
  only state where private-sector workers' compensation coverage is generally
  optional (Texas Labor Code ch. 406). A photo/video vendor with employees is not
  legally required to carry WC (though non-subscribers must report and lose
  certain defenses). Not encoded as a mandatory insurance obligation here; the
  elective-WC treatment lives in the `venue-org` dossier. (Labor Code chapter cited
  from statute structure; specific section not independently fetched this session.)
- **Entity / assumed-name registration.** Forming an LLC/corporation (Secretary of
  State) or filing an assumed name ("DBA") is an entity-formation matter, not an
  occupational license; noted for context, owned elsewhere.

## Drone conduct law (noted — creates NO Texas license/permit)

- **Texas Government Code ch. 423 (Use of Unmanned Aircraft) is a criminal
  *conduct* statute, not a licensing scheme.** It creates offenses for capturing
  images with a drone under specified circumstances; it does **not** require a
  drone operator or photographer to obtain any Texas license, permit, or
  registration. Operative text, § 423.003(a): "A person commits an offense if the
  person uses an unmanned aircraft to capture an image of an individual or
  privately owned real property in this state with the intent to conduct
  surveillance on the individual or property captured in the image." (Ch. 423 also
  provides numerous lawful-use exceptions in § 423.002.) A commercial event drone
  operator's Texas obligation is therefore behavioral (don't unlawfully surveil),
  with the only *document* obligations being FEDERAL (FAA Part 107 certificate +
  registration — see the federal file). Source: Tex. Gov't Code § 423.003; the
  official statutes site (statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/GV/htm/GV.423.htm) is a
  JavaScript SPA and could not be fetched by WebFetch or curl this session — text
  verified via the FindLaw faithful reproduction
  (https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/government-code/gov-t-sect-423-003/, fetched
  2026-07-07). Treated as a Note only; not encoded.
